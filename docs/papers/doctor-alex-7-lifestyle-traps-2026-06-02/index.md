## Papers and findings

### 1. Mediterranean diet and cardiovascular prevention

**Paper:** Estruch et al., “Primary Prevention of Cardiovascular Disease with a Mediterranean Diet Supplemented with Extra-Virgin Olive Oil or Nuts.” *New England Journal of Medicine* (2018).  
**Video theme:** Treat food as medicine.  
**PubMed:** <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29897866/>  
**DOI:** <https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1800389>  
**Repository PDF:** no open PDF verified.

**Findings:** In the revised PREDIMED randomized trial, 7,447 adults aged 55–80 at high cardiovascular risk but without baseline cardiovascular disease were assigned to a Mediterranean diet supplemented with extra-virgin olive oil, a Mediterranean diet supplemented with mixed nuts, or low-fat diet advice. Over a median 4.8 years, major cardiovascular events occurred in 3.8% of the olive-oil group, 3.4% of the nut group, and 4.4% of controls. Adjusted hazard ratios were about 0.69 for olive oil and 0.72 for nuts versus control, supporting fewer major cardiovascular events with Mediterranean dietary patterns. Important caveat: the original report was withdrawn and republished because of randomization/protocol deviations, so the revised analyses adjust for those issues rather than relying only on perfect randomization.

### 2. Ultra-processed foods and adverse health outcomes

**Paper:** Lane et al., “Ultra-processed food exposure and adverse health outcomes: umbrella review of epidemiological meta-analyses.” *BMJ* (2024).  
**Video theme:** Treat food as medicine / avoid industrialized diets.  
**PubMed:** <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38418082/>  
**DOI:** <https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj-2023-077310>  
**Repository PDF:** [PMID38418082 PDF](pdfs/PMID38418082_Ultra-processed_food_exposure_and_adverse_health_outcomes_umbrella_re.pdf)

**Findings:** This umbrella review synthesized 45 pooled analyses, including 13 dose-response and 32 non-dose-response associations, covering nearly 9.9 million participants. Higher ultra-processed food exposure was directly associated with 32 of 45 health parameters across mortality, cancer, mental, respiratory, cardiovascular, gastrointestinal, and metabolic outcomes. The strongest credibility categories supported associations with cardiovascular disease mortality, type 2 diabetes, prevalent anxiety outcomes, and common mental disorder outcomes. The paper is broad and useful for risk mapping, but much of the evidence is observational and many GRADE ratings were low or very low, so it supports risk association more strongly than definitive causation.

### 3. Exercise as an anti-inflammatory intervention

**Paper:** Gleeson et al., “The anti-inflammatory effects of exercise: mechanisms and implications for the prevention and treatment of disease.” *Nature Reviews Immunology* (2011).  
**Video theme:** Do hard things / regular movement.  
**PubMed:** <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21818123/>  
**DOI:** <https://doi.org/10.1038/nri3041>  
**Repository PDF:** no open PDF verified.

**Findings:** This review explains how regular exercise can lower chronic inflammatory burden, partly by reducing visceral fat and the inflammatory adipokines it releases, and partly through repeated acute exercise bouts that induce a more anti-inflammatory cytokine environment. The paper distinguishes beneficial regular activity from extreme training loads, noting that elite-level heavy exertion can suppress immunity and increase infection susceptibility. Its practical implication is that consistent, moderate-to-vigorous activity helps prevent and treat diseases with inflammatory components, including metabolic and cardiorespiratory disease.

### 4. Successful aging and controllable midlife factors

**Paper:** Vaillant & Mukamal, “Successful aging.” *American Journal of Psychiatry* (2001).  
**Video theme:** Lifestyle trajectory / long-term self-responsibility.  
**PubMed:** <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11384887/>  
**DOI:** <https://doi.org/10.1176/appi.ajp.158.6.839>  
**Repository PDF:** no open PDF verified.

**Findings:** This long follow-up study examined two male cohorts followed from adolescence for roughly 60 years or until death. It compared less-controllable predictors, such as parental social class, family cohesion, childhood temperament, ancestral longevity, major depression, and health at 50, with more modifiable predictors, including alcohol abuse, smoking, marital stability, exercise, BMI, coping mechanisms, and education. Variables assessed before age 50 predicted physical health, disability/death before 80, social supports, mental health, instrumental activities of daily living, and life enjoyment at ages 70–80. After accounting for the more controllable variables, depression was the only less-controllable factor that still predicted aging outcomes, suggesting substantial room for lifestyle and psychosocial factors to influence late-life health.

### 5. Social relationships and mortality risk

**Paper:** Holt-Lunstad, Smith & Layton, “Social relationships and mortality risk: a meta-analytic review.” *PLoS Medicine* (2010).  
**Video theme:** Remove isolation.  
**PubMed:** <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20668659/>  
**DOI:** <https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1000316>  
**Repository PDF:** [PMID20668659 PDF](pdfs/PMID20668659_Social_relationships_and_mortality_risk_a_meta-analytic_review..pdf)

**Findings:** This meta-analysis pooled 148 studies with 308,849 participants. Stronger social relationships were associated with a 50% greater likelihood of survival, and the association was consistent across age, sex, initial health status, cause of death, and follow-up duration. More complex measures of social integration had the strongest association with survival, while simple “living alone” measures were weaker. The authors conclude that social relationships predict mortality at a magnitude comparable with established risk factors.

### 6. Loneliness, social isolation, living alone, and mortality

**Paper:** Holt-Lunstad et al., “Loneliness and social isolation as risk factors for mortality: a meta-analytic review.” *Perspectives on Psychological Science* (2015).  
**Video theme:** Remove isolation.  
**PubMed:** <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25910392/>  
**DOI:** <https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691614568352>  
**Repository PDF:** no open PDF verified.

**Findings:** This meta-analysis examined mortality risk associated with objective social isolation, subjective loneliness, and living alone. After adjustment for possible confounders, social isolation was associated with 29% higher odds of mortality, loneliness with 26% higher odds, and living alone with 32% higher odds. The effects were broadly consistent by gender, follow-up length, and region, but initial health status and participant age moderated some results. The paper reinforces that both perceived and actual isolation are biologically meaningful risk factors, not just quality-of-life concerns.

### 7. Healthy lifestyle factors and life expectancy in the US

**Paper:** Li et al., “Impact of Healthy Lifestyle Factors on Life Expectancies in the US Population.” *Circulation* (2018).  
**Video theme:** Imagine doctors don’t exist / own the basics.  
**PubMed:** <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29712712/>  
**DOI:** <https://doi.org/10.1161/CIRCULATIONAHA.117.032047>  
**Repository PDF:** no open PDF verified.

**Findings:** Using long-term data from the Nurses’ Health Study and Health Professionals Follow-up Study, the authors modeled five low-risk lifestyle factors: never smoking, BMI 18.5–24.9, at least 30 minutes/day of moderate-to-vigorous physical activity, moderate alcohol intake, and high diet quality. Across up to 34 years and 42,167 deaths, adults with all five low-risk factors had much lower mortality than those with none: hazard ratio 0.26 for all-cause mortality, 0.35 for cancer mortality, and 0.18 for cardiovascular mortality. Estimated life expectancy at age 50 was 14.0 years longer for women and 12.2 years longer for men with all five factors versus none. The result is powerful but observational/model-based, so it should be read as an estimate of lifestyle-associated survival advantage rather than proof from a randomized trial.

### 8. Aerobic exercise, hippocampal size, and memory

**Paper:** Erickson et al., “Exercise training increases size of hippocampus and improves memory.” *PNAS* (2011).  
**Video theme:** Work on mobility / movement and brain health.  
**PubMed:** <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21282661/>  
**DOI:** <https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1015950108>  
**Repository PDF:** [PMID21282661 PDF](pdfs/PMID21282661_Exercise_training_increases_size_of_hippocampus_and_improves_memory..pdf)

**Findings:** In a randomized controlled trial of 120 older adults, aerobic exercise training increased anterior hippocampal volume by about 2%, effectively reversing roughly one to two years of age-related hippocampal volume loss. The increase was associated with improved spatial memory and higher serum BDNF, a mediator linked to neurogenesis. The control group’s hippocampal volume declined, while higher baseline fitness partly protected against decline. The study gives concrete neurobiological evidence that aerobic training can affect brain structure and memory-relevant function in late adulthood.

## Cross-paper synthesis

The cited papers converge on a simple preventive-health pattern: diet quality, regular exercise, social connection, and avoidance of major behavioral risks are repeatedly associated with lower chronic disease burden and longer survival. The strongest designs here include randomized trials for Mediterranean diet and aerobic exercise, while several lifestyle and social-connection papers are observational or meta-analytic and therefore more vulnerable to confounding. Even with that caveat, the direction is consistent: small daily behaviors compound over years, and social and metabolic health are not separate domains.
