# Intervention Watchlist

This page tracks interventions that are either high priority or commonly promoted in longevity circles.

## High-confidence, clinician-friendly topics

### Progressive resistance training

- Primary aim: preserve muscle mass, strength, glucose disposal, mobility, and independence.
- Watch for: orthopedic limitations, recovery, supervision needs.
- Evidence status: strong for function and healthy aging.

### Aerobic conditioning

- Primary aim: cardiovascular resilience, VO₂ max, metabolic health, cognition.
- Watch for: cardiac symptoms, overtraining, joint limitations.
- Evidence status: strong.

### Blood pressure and lipid optimization

- Primary aim: reduce cardiovascular and cerebrovascular risk.
- Watch for: overtreatment, orthostatic hypotension, polypharmacy.
- Evidence status: strong.

### SGLT2 inhibitors when clinically indicated

- Primary aim: cardiorenal and metabolic risk reduction in standard indications such as type 2 diabetes, heart failure, or chronic kidney disease.
- Why tracked: recent older-adult observational data suggest no excess geriatric-syndrome signal and lower hospitalization/mortality compared with DPP-4 inhibitors in type 2 diabetes.
- Watch for: volume depletion, genital/urinary infections, ketoacidosis risk in susceptible settings, renal-function thresholds, perioperative/sick-day medication management, and orthostatic symptoms.
- Evidence status: strong for indicated disease contexts; not a general longevity drug for people without indications.

### GLP-1 receptor agonists when clinically indicated

- Primary aim: obesity/diabetes management and cardiometabolic risk reduction in appropriate patients.
- Watch for: gastrointestinal effects, dehydration, gallbladder/pancreatitis concerns, excessive lean-mass loss during weight reduction, frailty/nutrition issues, and rare ocular safety signals such as possible semaglutide-associated NAION.
- Evidence status: strong for indicated obesity/diabetes contexts; safety monitoring remains important in older adults.

## Geroscience watchlist

### Rapamycin / rapalogs

- Why tracked: strong animal data and mechanistic rationale.
- Human longevity evidence: not yet definitive.
- Safety issues: immune effects, lipids, glucose, mouth ulcers, wound healing, dosing uncertainty.
- Repository stance: monitor trials and high-quality reviews; discuss only with an experienced physician.

### Metformin

- Why tracked: diabetes outcomes, possible aging-related associations.
- Human longevity evidence in non-diabetics: uncertain.
- Safety issues: B12 deficiency, GI effects, renal function considerations, possible exercise adaptation concerns.

### NAD precursors

- Why tracked: mechanistic interest and commercial popularity.
- Human outcome evidence: limited.
- Repository stance: require clinically meaningful outcomes, not just NAD-level changes.

### Senolytics

- Why tracked: promising preclinical rationale.
- Human evidence: early and indication-specific.
- Safety issues: varies by compound; avoid DIY protocols.
