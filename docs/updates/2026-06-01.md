## Included items

### 1. Combined influenza/COVID-19 mRNA vaccine durability in adults ≥50 years

- **Citation / URL:** Kostanyan L, Wu I, Sinkiewicz M, et al. *Safety and durability of influenza and SARS-CoV-2 antibody responses through 6 months after a single dose of mRNA-1083, a multicomponent influenza and COVID-19 vaccine, in adults ≥50 years.* Human Vaccines & Immunotherapeutics. 2026. PMID: 42213900. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42213900/
- **Date found:** 2026-06-01
- **Evidence type:** Phase 3, observer-blind, active-controlled randomized clinical trial; 6-month safety/reactogenicity and immunogenicity follow-up.
- **Population studied:** Adults aged ≥50 years, with a prespecified ≥65-year cohort, randomized to investigational combined mRNA-1083 influenza/COVID-19 vaccine or age-appropriate licensed comparator influenza and COVID-19 vaccines.
- **Rubric score:** 8.5/10 — high relevance to older adults, strong trial design, clinically relevant infection-resilience domain, but immunogenicity/safety rather than hard outcomes.
- **Summary:** A single dose of mRNA-1083 produced influenza hemagglutination-inhibition titers that were maintained through 6 months and generally comparable to or higher than comparator vaccines. SARS-CoV-2 neutralizing antibody responses remained higher than comparators through 6 months. Reactogenicity was mostly grade 1–2, vaccination-related unsolicited adverse events were similar between groups, and no vaccination-related serious adverse events or deaths were reported through 6 months.
- **Main finding:** A combined influenza/COVID-19 mRNA vaccine candidate showed durable humoral responses and acceptable 6-month safety in adults ≥50, including older adults.
- **Relevance to a 74-year-old male:** Respiratory infections are a major driver of hospitalization, functional decline, and cardiovascular stress in older men. This supports the broader evidence map category of age-appropriate vaccination and infection resilience, while noting that mRNA-1083 is investigational and not an instruction to choose a specific product.
- **Limitations:** Immunogenicity endpoints are not the same as confirmed reductions in hospitalization, mortality, frailty, or functional decline. Follow-up was 6 months; rare adverse events require larger post-authorization surveillance if licensed.
- **Safety considerations / clinician discussion:** Vaccination choices should be individualized with a clinician/pharmacist, especially for prior severe vaccine reactions, immune compromise, anticoagulation/injection concerns, or timing with other vaccines. This is not medical advice.
- **Evidence-map change:** Yes — added an infection-resilience category emphasizing age-appropriate vaccination as strong evidence, with combined flu/COVID mRNA products remaining product-specific/watchlist until outcome and authorization data mature.

### 2. Real-time continuous glucose monitoring in type 2 diabetes on basal insulin plus modern therapies

- **Citation / URL:** Wilmot EG, Moore P, Sathyapalan T, et al. *Continuous glucose monitoring versus self-monitoring of blood glucose in individuals with type 2 diabetes: a randomised, multicentre, open-label, superiority trial.* The Lancet Diabetes & Endocrinology. 2026. PMID: 42035781. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42035781/
- **Date found:** 2026-06-01
- **Evidence type:** Multicenter randomized controlled trial (FreeDM2), open-label, parallel-group superiority design.
- **Population studied:** 303 adults with type 2 diabetes treated with basal insulin plus SGLT2 inhibitors, GLP-1 receptor agonists, or dual GIP/GLP-1 receptor agonists; mean age 60.7 years; 67% male; baseline HbA1c about 8.8%.
- **Rubric score:** 8.0/10 — high cardiometabolic relevance, RCT design, male-majority cohort; age is somewhat younger than 74 and endpoint is glycemic control rather than mortality/function.
- **Summary:** Real-time CGM improved HbA1c compared with self-monitoring of blood glucose. Adjusted HbA1c differences favored CGM at 16 weeks (-0.6 percentage points; 95% CI -0.8 to -0.3) and 32 weeks (-0.5; 95% CI -0.7 to -0.2). Non-device-related adverse events were similar between groups; two severe hypoglycemia events occurred in the control group.
- **Main finding:** For adults with type 2 diabetes using basal insulin and modern cardiometabolic agents, CGM improved glycemic control compared with finger-stick self-monitoring.
- **Relevance to a 74-year-old male:** If type 2 diabetes and basal insulin are present, better glucose visibility may support safer titration and cardiometabolic risk management. For older adults, the relevance is strongest when balancing glycemic benefit against hypoglycemia risk, treatment burden, dexterity/vision barriers, and individualized HbA1c targets.
- **Limitations:** Open-label design; industry funding (Abbott Diabetes Care); mean age 60.7 rather than geriatric-specific; outcomes were HbA1c and safety over 32 weeks, not cardiovascular events, cognition, frailty, or mortality.
- **Safety considerations / clinician discussion:** CGM use and insulin titration should be clinician-guided. Older adults may need individualized targets to avoid hypoglycemia, alarm fatigue, overtreatment, or anxiety from data overload. This is not medical advice and does not imply changing diabetes therapy without clinical supervision.
- **Evidence-map change:** Yes — diagnostics/tracking now notes CGM as strong for selected insulin-treated type 2 diabetes contexts, not as a general longevity tracker.

### 3. SGLT2 inhibitor absolute-benefit model for primary heart-failure prevention in type 2 diabetes

- **Citation / URL:** Young KG, McGovern AP, Hopkins R, et al. *Precision Prescribing of SGLT2 Inhibitors in Individuals With Type 2 Diabetes for Primary Prevention of Heart Failure: Model Development and Validation Study.* Diabetes Care. 2026. PMID: 42160591. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42160591/
- **Date found:** 2026-06-01
- **Evidence type:** Clinical prediction model development and validation using trial meta-analysis estimates plus UK primary-care linked hospital/death records.
- **Population studied:** 57,368 SGLT2 inhibitor initiators and 111,673 comparator initiators with type 2 diabetes; focus on individuals without established ASCVD, heart failure, or chronic kidney disease.
- **Rubric score:** 7.8/10 — clinically meaningful cardiometabolic prevention question and large validation data, but not an RCT of the model and not older-male-specific.
- **Summary:** SGLT2 inhibitor use was associated with a lower risk of new-onset heart failure (HR 0.70; 95% CI 0.63–0.78), consistent with trial evidence. Relative benefit did not vary by baseline absolute heart-failure risk, but predicted 5-year absolute benefit ranged widely (<0.1% to 14.1%; median 1.0%), supporting more individualized targeting.
- **Main finding:** Absolute heart-failure benefit from SGLT2 inhibitors in type 2 diabetes varies substantially by baseline risk; a deployable model may better identify who is most likely to benefit for primary prevention.
- **Relevance to a 74-year-old male:** Older men with type 2 diabetes often have elevated heart-failure risk, but medication benefit should be framed as absolute benefit versus adverse-event risk. This reinforces clinician-led individualized prescribing rather than treating SGLT2 inhibitors as nonspecific longevity drugs.
- **Limitations:** Prediction model and observational validation; residual confounding possible; model transportability outside UK primary-care systems requires evaluation; does not directly address frailty, falls, genitourinary infection, volume depletion, or geriatric outcomes.
- **Safety considerations / clinician discussion:** SGLT2 inhibitors require clinician oversight, especially for kidney function, volume status, orthostatic symptoms, genital/urinary infections, ketoacidosis risk, perioperative/sick-day holds, and concurrent diuretics or antihypertensives. This is not medical advice.
- **Evidence-map change:** Minor — existing SGLT2 section already strong for indicated cardiometabolic contexts; wording updated to emphasize absolute-benefit targeting in type 2 diabetes.

### 4. Cardiovascular event timing after COVID-19 across variant eras

- **Citation / URL:** Howick JF V, Gersh BJ, Scott CG, et al. *Time-Critical Cardiovascular Risk After COVID-19: A Population-Based Analysis Across Variant Eras.* Mayo Clinic Proceedings: Innovations, Quality & Outcomes. 2026. PMID: 42158619. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42158619/
- **Date found:** 2026-06-01
- **Evidence type:** Population-based retrospective cohort study using linked medical records.
- **Population studied:** 162,471 adults with SARS-CoV-2 infection from March 2020 through December 2023 in a Minnesota/Wisconsin records-linkage system; age-stratified analyses including adults ≥80 years.
- **Rubric score:** 7.6/10 — large cohort and high relevance to older-adult infection/cardiovascular resilience; observational design and variant-era historical context limit causal inference.
- **Summary:** Major adverse cardiovascular events, thrombotic events, and dysrhythmias clustered most strongly within 30 days after infection. The early hazard was especially pronounced in older adults during pre-Delta and Delta eras; Omicron-era risk was lower but age-related dysrhythmia risk persisted.
- **Main finding:** COVID-19-associated cardiovascular events are concentrated in an early post-infection window, with older adults carrying the greatest absolute vulnerability.
- **Relevance to a 74-year-old male:** Reinforces infection prevention, vaccination discussions, and prompt clinician attention to cardiovascular symptoms after respiratory infection. It is most actionable as a monitoring/caution signal, not as a medication recommendation.
- **Limitations:** Retrospective observational study; variant eras, vaccination status, treatment availability, and testing behavior may confound results; strongest reported risk was in ≥80-year-olds rather than exactly age 74.
- **Safety considerations / clinician discussion:** Chest pain, dyspnea, palpitations, syncope, focal neurologic symptoms, or leg swelling after COVID-19 warrant clinical evaluation. Do not self-start anticoagulants, antiplatelets, or cardiac medications based on this study. This is not medical advice.
- **Evidence-map change:** Yes — infection-resilience section now notes post-infection cardiovascular vigilance as moderate observational evidence.
